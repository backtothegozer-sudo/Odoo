12 starter articles for your Odoo knowledge base.

Short, practical and operations-focused content to help your teams scope, deploy and improve ERP delivery without timeline drift.

FR EN

April 15, 2026 · 6 min · Scoping

1. How to start an Odoo project: scope in 3 workshops

A fast project kickoff is not always a good kickoff. The three workshops that matter are: business goals, real-life processes, and deployment priorities. The objective is not to debate every Odoo option, but to define what must work by day 30 after go-live.

In practice, we recommend one leadership workshop, one operations workshop, and one finance workshop. This structure aligns expectations, avoids late arbitration, and secures a deliverable first phase.

April 14, 2026 · 5 min · Architecture

2. Standard vs custom: making the decision without overspending

The reflex of "let's build custom" is often more expensive than expected, especially when standard configuration can solve the issue. A practical rule is to customize only when it creates a clear operational advantage or addresses a strong compliance constraint.

Before coding, write a mini business case: build cost, maintenance cost, user impact, and upgrade risk. This discipline prevents the accumulation of custom modules that provide little long-term value.

April 13, 2026 · 7 min · Data

3. Data migration: an anti-regression method

The quality of migrated data defines how much users trust the new ERP. A reliable migration uses three cycles: cleanup, test import, and business validation. Each cycle needs measurable expectations, explicit rejection criteria, and owner accountability.

The key is to assign one data owner per department. Without ownership, errors bounce between teams and resurface at go-live. With ownership, data quality improves quickly and stays under control.

April 12, 2026 · 6 min · Adoption

4. User adoption: avoiding the tunnel effect

Training users only at the end of the project is a common mistake. Adoption must start during prototyping with concrete, role-based scenarios and short feedback loops. The earlier teams test real workflows, the lower the rejection rate in production.

A practical setup combines targeted demos, team-specific quick guides, and weekly friction reviews. The goal is not to teach "all of Odoo", but to make critical daily tasks smooth and repeatable.

April 11, 2026 · 5 min · Integration

5. Connecting Odoo to e-commerce without technical debt

A strong e-commerce integration is more than order sync. You also need robust handling of statuses, refunds, stock availability, and logistics exceptions. Without this foundation, teams spend too much time fixing inconsistencies manually.

Prioritize critical flows first: order, payment, stock, invoice. Add retry logic and anomaly alerts from day one. This approach lowers support effort and protects growth as transaction volume increases.

April 10, 2026 · 6 min · Governance

6. Running a multi-company deployment

In a multi-company context, success depends on balancing standardization and local autonomy. The most robust model defines a common core (finance, master data, security) while allowing controlled local variants for operational processes.

Document what is global, what is local, and who decides when conflicts appear. Without clear governance, exceptions multiply, test cycles get heavier, and delivery timelines become unpredictable.

April 9, 2026 · 5 min · Finance

7. Automating invoicing while keeping accounting control

Automation is useful only when accounting rules are explicit and testable. Before enabling automatic invoices, secure VAT logic, exception handling, and validation workflows. Otherwise, you save time on entry but lose time in correction.

Keep controls lightweight but strict: variance logs, reconciliation views, and a weekly finance-operations review during the first production weeks.

April 8, 2026 · 6 min · Supply

8. Inventory and logistics: stabilizing flows in 6 weeks

Most stock issues do not come from Odoo itself; they come from gaps between documented processes and real warehouse behavior. To stabilize quickly, start with high-frequency flows: receiving, picking, shipping, and cycle counting.

Track three KPIs: theoretical-vs-actual stock variance, processing lead time, and picking anomaly rate. With weekly review and ownership, measurable gains usually appear within the first month.

April 7, 2026 · 5 min · Security

9. Security and access rights: building a robust model

An effective access model reflects real responsibilities, not an idealized org chart. Define clear role profiles, avoid individual one-off permissions, and trace sensitive approvals from the beginning.

Access reviews should be planned during implementation and repeated in run mode. This discipline reduces operational errors, fraud risk, and handover friction when teams change.

April 6, 2026 · 6 min · Performance

10. Odoo dashboards: 8 KPIs that actually matter

A useful dashboard is not a wall of numbers. For ERP execution, focus on a small set of decision KPIs: order cycle time, on-time delivery, gross margin, DSO, stock turns, support backlog, active adoption, and data quality.

Each KPI must have an owner and a defined corrective action. Without this action link, reporting may look reassuring but rarely improves operations.

April 5, 2026 · 7 min · Go-live

11. Planning a phased go-live without operational disruption

A phased go-live is often safer than a full cutover, especially when teams share core transactions. The key is to split by controllable scope: legal entity, product family, or process stream.

Build a precise cutover plan: who does what, at what time, and with which rollback criteria. This preparation significantly reduces stress and shortens critical incident windows.

April 4, 2026 · 5 min · Run

12. After go-live: support model and continuous improvement

ERP success is measured after launch. The first 90 days should include a clear operating model: incident triage, fit-for-purpose SLAs, enhancement prioritization, and monthly value reviews against business objectives.

This post-go-live governance turns a "finished project" into a living system that evolves with business needs. It is also the most reliable way to protect long-term Odoo ROI.
